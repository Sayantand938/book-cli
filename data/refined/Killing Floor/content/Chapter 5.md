Finlay leaned back in his chair. He was tall and graceful, with his arms behind his head. He was a well-educated, sophisticated, and experienced man from Boston. Now, he was sending me to jail for something I didn't do. He sat up straight, placing his open hands on the desk.

"I'm sorry, Reacher," he said.

"You're sorry?" I asked. "You're putting two innocent guys in jail and you're sorry?"

He shrugged, looking upset.

"This is what Chief Morrison wants," he explained. "He's decided it's over and wants to finish it before the weekend. He's the boss, after all."

"You can't be serious," I replied. "He's being an idiot. He's calling Stevenson, his own officer, a liar."

Finlay shrugged. "Not exactly. He thinks it might be a secret plan. Maybe Hubble wasn't actually there, but he got you to do it. He thinks the confession is stretched because Hubble might be scared of you and doesn't want to accuse you directly. Morrison believes you were going to Hubble's house to get paid when we caught you. He thinks that's why you waited eight hours and why Hubble was home from work, waiting to pay you."

I didn't say anything. I was concerned. Chief Morrison was a threat. His idea made sense, if Finlay hadn't checked the facts. If Finlay checked them at all.

"Reacher, I'm sorry," he said. "You and Hubble will have to stay locked up until Monday. You'll be okay. You'll be taken to Warburton. It's not a great place, but the holding cells are decent. It's much worse if you end up staying there for a longer time. In the meantime, I'll work on your case before Monday. I'll ask Officer Roscoe, the pretty officer outside, to come in on Saturday and Sunday. She's a good officer, our best. If you're telling the truth, you'll be free on Monday. Is that okay?"

I stared at him, getting angry.

"No, Finlay, it's not okay," I said. "You know I didn't do anything wrong. You know it wasn't me. You're just scared of that useless, fat guy, Morrison. So, I'm going to jail because you're a coward."

He took it calmly. His dark face turned redder. He sat quietly for a while. I took a deep breath and glared at him. My anger faded, and I just looked at him. I was back in control. Now it was his turn to glare at me.

"Two things, Reacher," he said, speaking clearly. "First, if I have to, I'll deal with Chief Morrison on Monday. Second, I'm not a coward. You don't know me at all."

I looked back at him. It was six o'clock, time for the prison bus.

"I know more than you think," I said. "I know you went to Harvard after college, you're divorced, and you stopped smoking in April."

Finlay looked surprised. Baker knocked and came in to say the prison bus was here. Finlay got up and walked around the desk. He told Baker he would bring me out himself. Baker went to get Hubble.

"How do you know that stuff?" Finlay asked.

He was curious. He was starting to lose.

"It's simple," I said. "You're smart, right? You said you went to school in Boston. But back when you were in college, Harvard didn't accept many black students. You're smart, but not a genius, so I'm guessing you got your first degree from Boston University, right?"

"Right," he admitted.

"And then you went to Harvard for your advanced degree," I said. "You did well at Boston University, things went well, and you got into Harvard. You sound like a Harvard graduate. I knew it right away. A Ph.D. in Criminology?"

"Right," he said again. "Criminology."

"And you started this job in April," I said. "You told me that. You get a retirement payment from the Boston Police Department because you worked there for twenty years. So you came here with extra money. But you came alone, because if you had a woman, she would have spent some of that money on new clothes for you. She probably hated that old-fashioned tweed suit you're wearing. She would have thrown it out and bought you a new, modern outfit to start your new life off well. But you're still wearing that terrible old suit, so the woman is gone. Either she died or you got divorced, so it was a fifty-fifty chance. Looks like I guessed correctly."

He just stared at me, blankly.

"And the smoking thing is easy," I said. "You were stressed and kept touching your pockets, looking for cigarettes. That means you quit not too long ago. I'm guessing you quit in April, with your new life and new job, no more cigarettes. You probably thought you'd quit now to avoid getting cancer."

Finlay looked at me, annoyed and reluctant.

"Very good, Reacher," he said. "Simple deduction, right?"

I shrugged and didn't say anything.

"So, figure out who killed the guy at the warehouse," he said.

"I don't care who killed anyone, anywhere," I replied. "That's your issue, not mine. And you're asking the wrong thing, Finlay. First, you need to find out who the dead guy was, right?"

"So, do you have any way to do that, smart guy?" he asked. "No ID, no face left, the fingerprints are useless, and Hubble isn't saying a thing?"

"Run the fingerprints again," I said. "I'm serious, Finlay. Have Roscoe do it."

"Why?" he asked.

"Something's not right," I said.

"What's not right?" he asked.

"Just run them again, okay?" I said. "Will you do that?"

He just grunted, not saying yes or no. I opened the office door and went out. Roscoe was gone. Nobody was there except Baker and Hubble by the jail cells. I could see the desk sergeant outside through the front doors. He was writing on a clipboard held by the prison bus driver. Behind them was the prison bus, parked in the curved driveway. It filled the view through the big glass entrance. It was a school bus painted light gray. It had "State of Georgia Department of Corrections" written along the side, under the windows. Below that was a crest. The windows had metal bars over them.

Finlay came out of the office after me. He touched my arm and led me over to Baker. Baker was holding three sets of handcuffs hooked over his thumb. They were bright orange, but the paint was chipped, showing the gray steel underneath. Baker put a pair of handcuffs on each of my wrists. Then, he unlocked Hubble's cell and told the scared banker to come out. Hubble looked blank and confused, but he came out. Baker took the loose handcuff on my left wrist and snapped it onto Hubble's right wrist. He put the third set of handcuffs on Hubble's other wrist. Ready to go.

"Take his watch, Baker," I told him. "He'll lose it in jail."

He nodded. He understood that someone like Hubble could lose a lot in jail. Baker took the expensive Rolex off Hubble's wrist. The bracelet wouldn't go over the handcuff, so Baker had to struggle to take the handcuff off and put it back on. The prison driver opened the door a bit and looked annoyed. He was in a hurry. Baker put Hubble's watch on the closest desk, right where my friend Roscoe had left her coffee cup.

"Okay, let's go," Baker said.

He walked us to the doors. We stepped out into the bright, hot sunshine. We were handcuffed together, which made walking difficult. Before getting on the bus, Hubble stopped and carefully looked around, more alert than Baker or the driver. Maybe he was worried about a neighbor seeing him, but nobody was there. We were far from the town, and I could see the church steeple in the distance. We walked to the bus in the warm evening air. The sun felt hot on my right cheek.

The driver opened the bus door. Hubble awkwardly stepped onto the bus, and I followed. Turning to walk down the aisle was clumsy. The bus was empty. The driver told Hubble to sit down, and he slid over to the window. I sat next to him. The driver knelt on the seat in front of us and locked our outer wrists to a metal bar that ran across the top of the seat. He shook each of the cuffs to make sure they were secure. I didn't blame him, having done that job before. It's terrible to drive with prisoners loose behind you.

The driver went to his seat and started the engine, which made a loud noise. The bus vibrated, and the air was hot and stuffy. There was no air conditioning, and the windows didn't open. I could smell fuel. The gears made grinding noises as the bus started moving. I looked out the window to my right. Nobody was waving goodbye.

We drove north out of the police parking lot, leaving the town behind and heading toward the highway. We passed Eno's diner, but the parking lot was empty. Nobody was there for an early dinner. We continued north for a while, then turned sharply left off the main road onto a road between fields. The bus settled into a noisy ride. We passed rows and rows of bushes and red soil. The sun was setting ahead of us, a giant red ball heading toward the fields. The driver had pulled down the sun visor, which had instructions on how to operate the bus.

Hubble rocked and bounced next to me, saying nothing. He had slumped down, his face looking at the floor. His left arm was raised because it was handcuffed to the metal bar. His right arm was limp between us. He still had his expensive sweater over his shoulders. Where his Rolex used to be, there was just pale skin. He seemed completely drained and paralyzed by fear.

WE CONTINUED ROCKING AND BOUNCING FOR ALMOST ANOTHER hour through the huge countryside. A small group of trees flashed past on my right. Then, in the distance, I saw a structure alone in a large flat area of farmland. Against the red setting sun, it looked like something from hell, forced up through the ground. It was a group of buildings that looked like a chemical factory or a nuclear plant, with massive concrete structures and metal walkways, pipes running everywhere with steam rising. The whole area was surrounded by fences and towers. As we got closer, I could see bright lights and razor wire, searchlights and rifles in the towers, and layers of fences separated by plowed red soil. Hubble didn't look up, and I didn't bother him. It wasn't a fun place ahead.

The bus slowed down as we approached. The outer fence was about a hundred yards away, creating a large boundary. It was a strong fence, about fifteen feet tall, with pairs of bright lights along its entire length. One light of each pair shone inward across the plowed ground, and the other shone outward over the surrounding farmland. All the lights were on, and the whole complex was brightly lit with yellow light, making the red soil look pale.

The bus stopped with a jerk. The engine was still running, making the bus shake. The air inside, which wasn't great to begin with, stopped circulating. It became very hot and stuffy. Finally, Hubble looked up. He looked out through his glasses with the gold frames. He glanced around and out the window. He made a sound like a hopeless sigh. Then he lowered his head again.

The driver was waiting for the guard at the first gate to give him the signal to go. The guard was talking on a radio. The driver revved the engine and shifted gears with a crunch. The guard waved them through, using his radio like a stick. The bus moved slowly into a fenced area. As they passed, they saw a long sign that read: Warburton Correctional Facility, State of Georgia Department of Corrections. The gate behind them slammed shut. They were now trapped inside a wire cage, even the roof was made of wire. At the other end, a gate opened. The bus moved forward.

They drove about a hundred yards to another fence. There was another fenced area for vehicles. The bus entered, paused, and then drove out. They were now in the center of the prison. They stopped in front of a concrete building. This was the reception area. The noise of the engine bounced off the concrete walls. Then the engine was turned off, and the shaking and noise stopped. The driver got out of his seat and walked down the aisle, bending low and using the tops of the seats to pull himself along. He took out his keys and unlocked the handcuffs that held them to the seats.

“Okay, guys, let’s go,” he said with a grin. “Party time.”

They got up from their seats and slowly walked to the front of the bus. Hubble tugged on Reacher’s arm. The driver stopped them. He removed all three sets of handcuffs and tossed them into a bin next to his seat. He pulled a lever and the door opened. They stepped off the bus. A door across from them opened and a guard came out. He called them over. He was eating a donut and talking with his mouth full. There was sugar on his upper lip. He seemed very relaxed. They went through the door into a small, dirty concrete room. There were cheap chairs around a painted table. Another guard was sitting on the table, reading from a worn-out clipboard.

“Sit down, okay?” he said. They sat. He stood up. The guard with the donut locked the door behind them and joined him.

“Here’s the deal,” the guard with the clipboard said. “You guys are Reacher and Hubble. You’re here from Margrave. You haven’t been found guilty of any crime. You’re being held while we investigate. You can’t ask to be released on bail. Do you understand what I’m saying? You haven’t been found guilty of any crime. That’s what’s important. It means you don’t have to do a lot of the things that prisoners do here, okay? No uniform, no processing, no big deal, you understand? You’ll have nice rooms on the top floor.”

“Right,” said the guard with the donut. “If you were convicts, we’d be searching you and messing with you, and you’d get a uniform, and we’d put you with the other prisoners and just watch what happens, right?”

“Right,” his partner said. “So, what we’re saying is this. We don’t want to make things difficult for you, so don’t make things difficult for us, okay? This place doesn’t have enough staff. The governor cut about half the staff to save money, okay? We have to balance the budget, right? So, we don’t have enough people to do things properly. We’re trying to do our jobs with half the people on every shift, right? So, what I’m saying is we’re going to put you in there, and we don’t want to see you again until we take you out on Monday. No trouble, right? We don’t have enough staff for trouble. We don’t have enough staff for trouble with the prisoners, let alone trouble with people being held, you understand? Hey, Hubble, do you understand?”

Hubble looked up at him and nodded without expression. He didn’t say anything.

"Reacher?" the man with the clipboard asked. "Do you understand?"

"Yes," I said. I understood. This guy didn't have enough staff and was having problems because he didn't have enough money. Meanwhile, his friends were getting unemployment benefits. Tell me something I don't know.

"Good," he said. "Here's the deal. We both get off work at seven o'clock, which is about a minute from now. We're not staying late for you guys. We don't want to, and the union won't let us anyway. So, you'll get a meal, and then you're locked in here until there are enough guards to take you upstairs. There won't be enough guards until after lights out, maybe around ten o'clock, okay? But no guards move prisoners around after lights out, right? The union won't allow it. So, Mr. Spivey himself will come get you. He's the assistant warden and the boss tonight. Around ten o'clock, okay? If you don't like it, don't tell me. Tell the governor, okay?"

The man who ate the donut went out to the hallway and came back a minute later with a tray. On it were covered plates, paper cups, and a thermos. He put the tray on the table, and the two of them left through the hallway. They locked the door from the outside. It became as quiet as a tomb in there.

We ate. Fish and rice. Friday food. Coffee in the thermos. Hubble didn't say anything. He left most of the coffee for me. That was nice of Hubble. I put the dirty dishes on the tray and the tray on the floor. Three more hours to waste. I leaned my chair back and put my feet up on the table. Not comfortable, but the best I was going to get. It was a warm evening in September in Georgia.

I looked at Hubble without much interest. He was still quiet. I had only heard him speak on Finlay's speakerphone. He looked back at me. He seemed sad and scared. He looked at me like I was a strange creature. He stared at me like I worried him. Then he looked away.

MAYBE I SHOULDN'T GO BACK TO THE GULF. BUT IT WAS too late in the year to go north. It's too cold up there. Maybe I should just go straight to the islands, like Jamaica. They have good music there. A hut on the beach. I could spend the winter in a hut on a Jamaican beach. Smoke a lot of marijuana every week. Do whatever people in Jamaica do. Maybe smoke even more marijuana with someone to share the hut with. Roscoe kept popping into my thoughts. Her uniform shirt looked really good, a neat, tight blue shirt. I had never seen a shirt look better. On a Jamaican beach in the sun, she wouldn't need a shirt. I didn't think that would be a problem.

It was her wink that got to me. She took my coffee cup, said I had nice eyes, and then she winked. That has to mean something, right? The eyes thing, I've heard that before. An English girl I used to hang out with liked my eyes. She said it all the time. They're blue. Other people have said they look like icebergs in the Arctic Ocean. If I focus, I can stop them from blinking, which makes my stare look intimidating. That's useful. But Roscoe's wink was the best part of the day. The only good part, really, except for Eno's scrambled eggs, which weren't bad. You can get eggs anywhere. But I'd miss Roscoe. I just went through the empty evening.

NOT LONG AFTER TEN, THE DOOR FROM THE HALLWAY WAS unlocked. A uniformed man came in. He was holding a clipboard and a shotgun. I looked him over. He was a Southerner, a big, fleshy man with reddish skin, a large belly, and a wide neck. He had small eyes. His uniform was tight and greasy, trying to hold him in. He was probably born right there on the farm they took over to build the prison. Assistant Warden Spivey, the boss of this shift. He was understaffed and stressed, so he was escorting the prisoners by himself with a shotgun in his big, red farmer hands.

He looked at his clipboard.

"Which one of you is Hubble?" he asked.

His voice was high and thin, which didn't fit his big size. Hubble raised his hand for a moment, like a kid in school. Spivey looked him up and down quickly, like a snake. He made a noise and pointed with his clipboard. We lined up and started walking. Hubble looked calm and did what he was told, like a tired soldier.

"Turn left and follow the red line," Spivey said.

He pointed left with his shotgun. There was a red line painted on the wall at about waist level. It was to show the way to the fire exit. I guessed it led outside, but we were going the wrong way. We were going into the prison, not out. We followed the red line through hallways, up stairs, and around corners. Hubble went first, then me, then Spivey with the shotgun. It was very dark, with only dim emergency lights on. Spivey stopped us on a landing. He used his key to open an electronic lock. This lock would open the fire door if there was an alarm.

"No talking," he said. "The rules here say everyone must be completely quiet after the lights go out. The cell is at the end on the right."

We walked through the open door. The terrible smell of the prison hit me. It was the smell of many sad men at night. It was almost completely dark. A night-light glowed softly. I could feel, more than see, rows of cells. I heard the quiet sounds of the night: breathing, snoring, mumbling, and crying. Spivey led us to the end of the row and pointed to an empty cell. We went inside. Spivey slammed the bars shut behind us. They locked by themselves. He walked away.

The cell was very dark. I could just see a bunk bed, a sink, and a toilet. There wasn't much space to walk. I took off my coat and threw it onto the top bunk. I reached up and turned the pillow so it wasn't near the bars. I liked it better that way. The sheet and blanket were worn, but they smelled clean enough.

Hubble sat quietly on the bottom bed. I used the toilet and washed my face in the sink. I climbed into the top bunk and took off my shoes. I left them at the end of the bed. I wanted to know where they were. Shoes can get stolen, and these were good shoes. I bought them many years ago in Oxford, England. That's a college town near the air base where I was working. They were big, heavy shoes with strong soles.

The bed was too short for me, but most beds are. I lay there in the dark and listened to the sounds of the restless prison. Then I closed my eyes and started thinking about Jamaica with Roscoe. I must have fallen asleep there with her because the next thing I knew, it was Saturday. I was still in prison. And an even worse day was starting.
