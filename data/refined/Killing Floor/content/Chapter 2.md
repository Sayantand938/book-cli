We arrived at the building. It was long and not very tall. Baker got out of the car and looked around. The other officers stayed close. Stevenson went behind our car and stood opposite Baker, pointing his shotgun at me. They were a good team. Baker opened my door.

"Okay, let's go," he said quietly.

He seemed nervous, looking all around. I turned slowly and got out of the car. The handcuffs made it harder. It was still hot. I walked forward and waited. The other officers walked behind me. In front of me was the police station entrance. Above the doors, it said "Town of Margrave Police Headquarters" in carved letters. The doors were made of thick glass. Baker opened one. It made a sucking sound because of the tight seals. The other officers pushed me through. The door closed behind me with the same sound.

Inside, it was cool. Everything was white and shiny. The lights were bright. It looked like a bank. There was carpet. A police officer stood behind a long desk. The place looked so fancy, he should have said, "How can I help you?" But he didn't say anything. He just looked at me. Behind him was a large, open area. A woman in uniform was sitting at a desk. She was working on a computer. Now she was looking at me. I stood there with an officer on each side of me. Stevenson was standing near the front desk, still pointing his shotgun at me. Baker was just looking at me. The police officer and the woman were looking at me. I looked back at them.

Then they walked me to the left and stopped in front of a door. Baker opened it, and I was pushed into a room. It was a room for questioning people. There were no windows. There was a white table and three chairs. There was carpet. In the corner of the room, there was a camera. The room was very cold. I was still wet from the rain.

I stood there while Baker took everything out of my pockets. My things made a small pile on the table. There was money, coins, receipts, and small pieces of paper. Baker looked at the newspaper and left it in my pocket. He glanced at my watch and left it on my wrist. He wasn't interested in those things. Everything else was put into a large plastic bag that could be sealed. It was a bag made for people with more things than I had. The bag had a white space on it. Stevenson wrote a number on it.

Baker told me to sit down. Then they all left the room. Stevenson took the bag with my stuff. They went out, closed the door, and I heard the lock click. It was a strong, smooth sound. The sound of a big metal lock. It sounded like a lock that would keep me locked up.

I THOUGHT THEY WOULD LEAVE ME ALONE FOR A WHILE. That's what usually happens. Being alone makes you want to talk. Wanting to talk can make you want to confess. Arresting someone roughly and then leaving them alone for an hour is a good plan.

I was wrong about their plan. They weren't just isolating me for an hour. Maybe that was their second mistake. Baker unlocked the door and came back in, holding a plastic cup of coffee. Then he signaled the woman in uniform to enter. She was the one I had seen at her desk. The heavy lock clicked shut behind her. She carried a metal case and placed it on the table. She opened it and took out a long black number holder with white plastic numbers.

She handed it to me with a mix of politeness and pity, like a dental nurse. I took it with my hands still cuffed. I looked closely to make sure it was right-side up and held it under my chin. The woman took an old camera out of the case and sat across from me. She leaned forward, resting her elbows on the table to steady the camera. Her breasts touched the edge of the table. She was an attractive woman with dark hair and beautiful eyes. I stared at her and smiled. The camera clicked and flashed. Before she could ask, I turned sideways and held the number against my shoulder for a profile shot. The camera clicked and flashed again. I turned back and held out the number with both hands because of the cuffs. She took it with a tight smile that said, "This is unpleasant, but necessary," just like a dental nurse.

Next, she took out the fingerprint equipment: a clean ten-card, already labeled with a number. The thumb spaces were too small. The card had a reverse side with two squares for palm prints. The handcuffs made it difficult. Baker didn't offer to remove them. The woman inked my hands. Her fingers were smooth and cool. She wasn't wearing a wedding ring. Afterward, she gave me a wad of tissues. The ink came off easily. It was a new type I hadn't seen before.

The woman took the film out of the camera and placed it with the fingerprint card on the table. She packed the camera back into the case. Baker knocked on the door. The lock clicked again. The woman picked up her things. No one spoke. She left the room, and Baker stayed with me. He shut the door, and it locked with the same smooth click. Then he leaned against the door and looked at me.

"My boss is coming down," he said. "You need to talk to him. We have a situation that needs to be cleared up."

I didn't reply. Talking wouldn't solve anything for anyone. But Baker was being polite and respectful, so I tested him by holding out my hands, silently asking him to remove the cuffs. He hesitated for a moment, then took out the key and unlocked them. He clipped the cuffs back onto his belt and looked at me. I looked back and lowered my arms. I didn't show any relief or rub my wrists. I didn't want a connection with him. But I did speak.

"Okay," I said. "Let's go meet your boss."

It was the first time I had spoken since ordering breakfast. Now Baker seemed grateful. He knocked twice on the door, and it was unlocked from the outside. He opened it and motioned for me to go through. Stevenson was waiting with his back to the large open area. The shotgun and backup crew were gone. Things were calming down. Two officers stood on either side of me. Baker lightly held my elbow. We walked down the side of the open area to a door at the back. Stevenson pushed it open, and we entered a large office with rosewood furniture.

A fat man sat at a big rosewood desk. Behind him were two large flags: the American flag with a gold fringe on the left and what I guessed was the Georgia state flag on the right. Between the flags was a large, round mahogany clock. It looked old and well-polished. I assumed it was from the old station house that had been torn down to build this new one. I figured the architect used it to give the new building a sense of history. It was almost 12:30.

The fat man looked up as I was led toward him. He seemed confused, as if he didn't recognize me. He looked again, more closely. Then he sneered at me and spoke in a strained, wheezing voice that would have been a shout if his lungs weren't so bad.

"Sit down and shut up," he ordered.

This heavy man was unexpected. He seemed like a jerk, unlike the others I had met. Baker and his team knew what they were doing. They were professional and quick. The fingerprint lady was nice. But this fat police chief seemed useless. He had thin, dirty hair and was sweating even though it was cold. His face was red and blotchy, showing he was unhealthy and overweight. He probably had high blood pressure and hard arteries. He didn't seem capable at all.

"My name is Morrison," he said, breathing heavily. As if I cared. "I'm the police chief here in Margrave. You're a murdering outsider. You came to my town and made trouble on Mr. Kliner's land. Now you're going to confess everything to my detective."

He paused and looked up at me, as if he was still trying to figure me out or waiting for me to say something. I didn't. So, he pointed a fat finger at me.

"Then you're going to jail," he said. "And then you're going to be executed. And then I'm going to disrespect your poor, insignificant grave."

He struggled to get out of his chair and looked away from me.

"I'd handle this myself," he said, "but I'm busy."

He moved slowly from behind his desk. I was standing between his desk and the door. As he squeezed past me, he stopped. His fat nose was about even with the middle button on my coat. He was still looking up at me as if he was confused.

"I've seen you before," he said. "Where was it?"

He looked at Baker and then at Stevenson, as if he wanted them to remember what he was saying and when he was saying it.

"I've seen him before," he said to them.

He slammed the office door, and I waited with the two police officers. Then, the head of detectives came in. He was a tall Black man, not old, but his hair was turning gray, and he was losing some. It made him seem important and classy. He was quick, sure of himself, and well-dressed in an old-style tweed suit. He also wore a moleskin vest and shiny shoes. He looked exactly how a chief should look. He told Baker and Stevenson to leave the office and closed the door behind them. He sat down at his desk and pointed to the chair across from him, telling me to sit there.

He quickly opened a drawer and took out a cassette recorder. He held it up high to untangle the cords. He plugged in the power and the microphone, put in a tape, and pressed record. Then, he tapped the microphone with his fingernail. He stopped the tape and rewound it. He pressed play and listened to the sound of his fingernail. He nodded, rewound the tape again, and pressed record. I just sat there and watched him.

For a moment, it was quiet. There was only a soft hum from the air, the lights, or the computer. Or maybe it was the recorder slowly running. I could hear the slow ticking of the old clock. It sounded patient, like it was ready to keep ticking forever, no matter what I did. Then, the man leaned back in his chair and looked at me closely. He put his fingertips together, like sophisticated people do.

"Okay," he said. "We have a few questions, don't we?"

His voice was deep, like a low rumble. He didn't have a Southern accent. He looked and sounded like a banker from Boston, except he was Black.

"My name is Finlay," he said. "I'm a captain, and I'm the chief of the detective unit here. I understand you've been told about your rights. But you haven't said that you understand them. Before we continue, we need to make sure you understand your rights."

Not just a Boston banker, more like someone from Harvard.

"I understand my rights," I said.

"Good," he said. "I'm happy to hear that. Where is your lawyer?"

"I don't need a lawyer," I replied.

"You're accused of murder," he said. "You need a lawyer. We can give you one for free. Do you want us to give you a free lawyer?"

"No, I don't need a lawyer," I repeated.

The man, Finlay, looked at me for a while. He was looking over his fingers.

"Okay," he said. "But you have to sign a paper saying you don't want a lawyer. It will say that we told you that you can have a lawyer for free, but you don't want one."

"Okay," I said.

He took out a form from a drawer and looked at his watch to write the date and time. He pushed the form towards me. There was a big 'X' on the line where I needed to sign. He gave me a pen. I signed it and pushed the form back to him. He looked at it and put it in a yellow folder.

"I can't read your signature," he said. "So, let's start with your name, your address, and your birthday, just to be clear for the record."

It was quiet again. I looked at him. He was a determined man. Probably around forty-five years old. You don't become the head of detectives in Georgia at forty-five if you're black unless you're very determined. There was no point in messing with him. I took a deep breath.

"My name is Jack Reacher," I said. "I don't have a middle name, and I don't have an address."

He wrote it down. There wasn't much to write. I told him when I was born.

"Okay, Mr. Reacher," Finlay said. "Like I said, we have many questions. I looked quickly through your things. You didn't have any ID with you. No driver's license, no credit cards, nothing. You say you don't have an address. So, I'm wondering, who are you?"

He didn't wait for me to say anything about that.

"Who was the man with the shaved head?" he asked.

I didn't answer. I was looking at the big clock, waiting for the minute hand to move.

"Tell me what happened," he said.

I had no idea what happened. None at all. Something happened to someone, but not to me. I just sat there and didn't answer.

"What does 'Pluribus' mean?" Finlay asked.

I looked at him and shrugged.

"The motto of the United States?" I asked. "E Pluribus Unum? The Second Continental Congress chose it in 1776, right?"

He just made a noise. I kept looking right at him. I thought he seemed like someone who might answer a question.

"What's this all about?" I asked him.

Again, he didn't say anything. He looked at me now. I could tell he was deciding if he should answer, and how he should do it.

"What's this about?" I asked him again. He leaned back and put his fingertips together.

"You know what this is about," he said. "Murder. And it's very strange. The dead person was found this morning at the Kliner warehouse. It's on the north end of the county road, near the highway. Someone saw a man walking away from there. It was a little after eight this morning. They said it was a tall, white man with light hair, wearing a long black coat, with no hat or bag."

Again, he was quiet. I'm a white man. I'm very tall. My hair is light. I was sitting there wearing a long black coat. I didn't have a hat or a bag. I had been walking on the county road for almost four hours this morning. From eight o'clock until about eleven forty-five.

"How long is the county road?" I asked. "From the highway all the way to here?"

Finlay thought for a moment.

"Maybe fourteen miles, I think," he said.

"Okay," I said. "I walked all the way from the highway to this town. It's about fourteen miles. A lot of people must have seen me. But that doesn't mean I hurt anyone."

He didn't answer. I was starting to wonder what was going on.

"Is that where you live?" I asked. "All the way near the highway?"

"Yes," he said. "It's clear who's in charge here. There's no way you can get out of this, Mr. Reacher. The town's area goes out fourteen miles, right to the highway. Those warehouses out there are mine to control, that's for sure."

He paused. I nodded. He kept talking.

"Kliner built that place five years ago," he said. "Have you heard of him?"

I shook my head.

"Why would I have heard of him?" I asked. "I've never been here before."

"He's important around here," Finlay said. "His business out there pays a lot of taxes, which helps us a lot. It brings in a lot of money and good things for the town without causing much trouble, because it's so far away, right? So, we try to look after it for him. But now someone's been killed there, and you need to explain yourself."

This guy was doing his job, but he was wasting my time.

"Okay, Finlay," I said. "I'll tell you everything I did from the moment I came into your awful town until they dragged me in here while I was eating breakfast. If you can figure anything out from that, I'll give you a prize. Because all I did was walk for almost four hours in the heavy rain through your fourteen terrible miles."

That was the longest I had spoken in six months. Finlay just stared at me. I watched him struggle with a common problem for detectives. He felt that I might not be the right person. But I was right there in front of him. So, what should he do? I let him think. I wanted to help him in the right direction at the right time. I was going to tell him that the real guy was still out there while he was wasting time with me. That would make him doubt himself. But he spoke first, and he was wrong.

"No statements," he said. "I'll ask the questions, and you'll answer them. You're Jack-nobody-Reacher. No address. No ID. What are you, a homeless person?"

I sighed. It was Friday. The clock showed it was already after noon. This guy Finlay was going to do everything by the book. I was going to spend the weekend in jail and probably get out on Monday.

"I'm not homeless, Finlay," I said. "I'm a traveler. It's very different."

He slowly shook his head.

"Don't be a smart aleck, Reacher," he said. "You're in big trouble. Something bad happened up there. Our witness saw you leaving the place. You're a stranger with no ID and no story. So don't be a smart aleck."

He was still just doing his job, but he was still wasting my time.

"I wasn't leaving a murder scene," I said. "I was walking down a road. There's a difference, right? People leaving murder scenes run and hide. They don't walk straight down the road. What's wrong with walking down a road? People walk down roads all the time, don't they?"

Finlay leaned forward and shook his head.

"No," he said. "Nobody walks that road anymore because everyone drives cars. So, why don't you have an address? Where are you from? Answer the questions. Let's finish this quickly."

"Okay, Finlay, let's get this done," I said. "I don't have an address because I don't live in one place. Maybe someday I will, and then I'll have an address. I'll even send you a postcard so you can put it in your precious address book since you care so much about it."

Finlay looked at me, thinking about what to do next. He decided to be patient. Patient, but still determined. It was like he wouldn't give up.

"Where are you from?" he asked. "What was your last address?"

"What do you mean by 'where am I from'?" I asked.

He tightened his lips. I was starting to annoy him. But he stayed calm, although his patience now had a cold, sarcastic edge.

"Okay," he said. "You don't understand my question, so let me make it very clear. I mean, where were you born, or where have you lived for most of your life, the place you think of as your home when you're talking to people?"

I just stared at him.

"I'll give you an example," he said. "I was born in Boston, went to school in Boston, and worked there for twenty years. So, I would say I'm from Boston, and I think you'd agree."

I knew it. He was a Harvard guy. A Harvard guy who was starting to lose his cool.

"Okay," I said. "You've asked what you wanted to ask. I'll answer. But listen to me. I'm not the right person for you. By Monday, you'll realize I'm not. So, do yourself a favor and keep searching for someone else."

"I appreciate your advice," he said. "And that you care about my job."

"Okay," I said. "According to your complicated explanation, I don't really have a hometown. I come from the military. I was born at an American army base in West Berlin. My father was in the Marine Corps, and my mother was a French woman he met in Holland. They got married in Korea."

"I was a military child," I said. "If you show me a list of all the U.S. bases around the world, that's a list of places I've lived. I went to high school in many different countries, and I spent four years at West Point."

"I stayed in the army," I said. "Military Police. I worked and lived at all those bases again. Then, Finlay, after thirty-six years of being first an officer's child and then an officer myself, suddenly there's no need for a big army because the Soviets collapsed. So, great, we get the peace benefit. Which means your taxes are spent on something else, but for me, it means I'm a thirty-six-year-old, jobless, former military policeman being called a bum by arrogant civilians who couldn't survive five minutes in the world I lived in."

He paused, thinking. He didn't seem interested.

"Go on," he told me.

"Right now, I'm just having fun," I said. "Maybe I'll find a job later, or maybe not. Maybe I'll find a place to live for good, or maybe I won't. But I'm not trying to find anything right now."

"When did you leave the army?" he asked.

"Six months ago," I answered. "In April."

"Are you kidding me?" I replied. "When was the last time you looked for a job?"

"April," he repeated. "Six months ago. That's when I got this job."

"That's great, Finlay," I replied.

I didn't know what else to say. Finlay looked at me for a short time.

"How have you been living?" he asked. "What was your rank?"

"Major," I answered. "They give you money when they let you go. I still have most of it. I'm trying to make it last as long as I can."

"OKAY, LET'S TALK ABOUT WHAT YOU'VE DONE IN THE LAST DAY," he said.

I sighed. I knew I was going to be in trouble now.

"I took the Greyhound bus," I said. "I got off at the county road. That was at eight this morning. I walked into town, went to that diner, ordered breakfast, and your people showed up and took me in while I was eating."

"Do you have a reason to be here?" he asked.

I shook my head.

"I don't have a job," I said. "I don't have any work anywhere."

He wrote that down.

"Where did you get on the bus?" he asked.

"In Tampa," I said. "It left last night at midnight."

"Tampa in Florida?" he asked.

I nodded. He quickly opened another drawer. He took out a Greyhound bus timetable. He flipped it open and moved his finger down the page. This guy was very careful. He looked at me.

"That's a fast bus," he said. "It goes straight north to Atlanta. It gets there at nine in the morning. It doesn't stop here at eight."

I shook my head.

"I asked the driver to stop," I said. "He said he wasn't supposed to, but he did. He stopped just for me and let me off."

"Have you been here before?" he asked.

I shook my head again.

"Do you have family living around here?" he asked.

"Not here," I replied.

"Do you have family anywhere?" he asked.

"I have a brother who lives in Washington, D.C.," I said. "He works for the government."

"Do you have any friends here in Georgia?" he asked.

"No," I answered.

Finlay wrote everything down. Then it was quiet for a while. I knew what he was going to ask next.

"So why?" he asked. "Why did you get off the bus where it wasn't supposed to stop and walk fourteen miles in the rain to a place you had no reason to go?"

That was the key question. Finlay had figured it out quickly. A lawyer would ask the same thing. And I didn't have a good answer.

"What can I say?" I replied. "It was a random choice. I just couldn't sit still. I needed to be somewhere, didn't I?"

"But why this place?" he asked.

"I don't know," I said. "The man next to me had a map, and I just pointed to this spot. I wanted to get away from the main roads. I thought I could go back down towards the Gulf, maybe further west."

"You just picked this place?" Finlay said. "Don't give me that nonsense. How could you just pick it? It's just a name, a little mark on the map. You must have had a reason."

I nodded.

"I thought I'd come here to look for Blind Blake," I said.

"Who in the world is Blind Blake?" he asked.

I watched him think, like a computer figuring out the possibilities. Was Blind Blake my friend, my enemy, someone I worked with, someone who helped me, someone I owed money to, someone who owed me money, or someone I was going to hurt?

"Blind Blake was a guitar player," I said. "He died about sixty years ago, maybe killed. My brother bought one of his records. The notes on the record said he died in Margrave. My brother wrote to me about it. He said he'd passed through here a few times in the spring for some kind of work. I thought I'd come and see if the story was true."

Finlay looked confused. It probably sounded like a weak story to him. I would have thought so too, if I were him.

"You came here to find someone who plays guitar?" he asked.

"A guitar player who died sixty years ago? Why do you want to find him? Do you play guitar?"

"No," I replied.

"How did your brother write to you?" he asked. "If he didn't have your address?"

"He sent the letter to my old army group," I said. "They send my mail to my bank, where I keep my money from when I left the army. The bank sends it to me when I ask them to send me money."

He shook his head and wrote something down.

"You took the midnight bus from Tampa, right?" he asked.

I nodded.

"Do you have your bus ticket?" he asked.

"I think it's in the bag with my things," I said. I remembered Baker putting all the things from my pockets in a bag and Stevenson labeling it.

"Do you think the bus driver will remember?" Finlay asked.

"Maybe," I replied. "It was a special stop. I had to ask him to stop there."

I started feeling like I was just watching. The situation felt distant and not real. My job wasn't much different from Finlay's. I had a strange feeling, like I was talking to him about someone else's problem. It was like we were coworkers talking about a tricky case.

"Why aren't you working?" Finlay asked.

I shrugged and tried to explain.

"Because I don't want to," I said. "I worked for thirteen years, and it didn't get me anywhere. I feel like I tried doing things their way, but forget them. Now I'm going to do things my way."

Finlay sat and stared at me.

"Did you have any problems in the army?" he asked.

"No more than you had in Boston," I said.

He looked surprised.

"What do you mean?" he asked.

"You said you lived in Boston for twenty years, Finlay," I replied. "So, why are you here in this small, unimportant town? You should be retired, fishing somewhere like Cape Cod. What's really going on with you?"

"That's my own problem, Mr. Reacher," he answered. "Answer my question."

I just lifted my shoulders.

"Ask the army," I told him.

"I will," he said. "You can be sure I'll do that. Did you leave the army with a good record?"

"Would they have paid me if I didn't?" I asked.

"Why should I believe they gave you any money?" he questioned. "You live like a homeless person. Did you have a good record when you left the army? Yes or no?"

"Yes," I responded. "Of course."

He wrote something else down and paused to think.

"How did you feel when they fired you?" he asked.

I thought for a moment and then shrugged.

"It didn't really make me feel anything," I said. "It's like I was in the army, and now I'm not."

"Are you angry?" he asked. "Disappointed?"

"No," I said. "Should I be?"

"You don't have any problems at all?" he asked, as if there had to be something wrong.

I felt like I needed to give him some kind of answer, but I couldn't think of anything. I had been in the service my whole life. Now I was out. Being out felt wonderful. It felt like freedom. It was like I had a small headache my whole life, but I didn't notice it until it was gone. My only problem was how to make a living. Figuring out how to make money without losing my freedom was going to be hard. I hadn't made any money in six months. That was my only problem. But I wasn't going to tell Finlay that. He would think it was a reason to suspect me. He'd think I decided to pay for my homeless life by robbing people, at warehouses, and then killing them.

"I guess it's hard to adjust," I said. "Especially since I had that kind of life as a kid too."

Finlay nodded, thinking about my answer.

"Why you specifically?" he said. "Did you ask to leave the service early?"

"I never offer to do anything," I said. "It's a soldier's basic rule."

It was quiet again.

"Did you have a special job?" he asked. "When you were in the army?"

"At first, I did general jobs," I said. "That's how it works. Then for five years, I was in charge of keeping secrets safe. After that, for the last six years, I did something else."

I waited for him to ask.

"What was that?" he asked.

"I investigated murders," I said.

Finlay leaned back in his chair. He made a noise. He put his fingertips together like a steeple again. He stared at me and breathed out. Then he leaned forward. He pointed at me.

"Okay," he said. "I'm going to check your story. We have your fingerprints. The army should have them too. We'll get your army record. Everything about it. All the details. We'll check with the bus company. Check your ticket. Find the driver and the passengers. If you're telling the truth, we'll know soon. And if it's true, it might help you get out of trouble. Of course, some details about when things happened and how you did things will decide what happens. But we don't know those details yet."

He stopped and breathed out again. He looked straight at me.

"In the meantime, I'm careful," he said. "You don't look good. You're like someone who wanders around with no home. You have no address, and we don't know anything about you. Your story might be a lie. You could be running from the law. You might have been killing people everywhere. I just don't know. I can't just trust you. Why should I even doubt anything right now? You stay locked up until we know the truth, okay?"

That's what I thought would happen. It's what I would have said if I were him. I just looked at him and shook my head.

"You're a cautious guy?" I said. "That's for sure."

He looked back at me.

"If I'm wrong, I'll buy you lunch on Monday," he said. "At Eno's restaurant, to make up for today."

I shook my head again.

"I'm not looking for a friend here," I said.

Finlay just shrugged. He turned off the tape recorder. Rewound it. Took out the tape. Wrote on it. He used the intercom on the big desk to call Baker back in. I waited. It was still cold. But I had finally dried off. The rain had poured down from the sky and soaked me. Now the dry air in the office had sucked the water out of me. A machine had taken the moisture out of the air and sent it somewhere else.

Baker knocked and came in. Finlay told him to take me to the cells. Then he nodded to me. It was a nod that meant: If you're not the bad guy, remember I was just doing my job. I nodded back. My nod meant: While you're protecting yourself, there's a killer out there.

THE CELL BLOCK WAS REALLY JUST A WIDE AREA NEXT TO the main office. It was divided into three separate cells with bars. The front wall was all bars. A gate opened into each cell. The metal looked shiny and expensive. Like titanium. Each cell had a carpet. But it was completely empty. No furniture or bed. Just a fancy version of the old holding cells you used to see.

"Can't I stay here tonight?" I asked Baker.

"No way," he said. "You're going to the state prison later. The bus comes at six. It'll bring you back on Monday."

He slammed the gate shut and locked it. I heard the locks click into place. They were electric. I took the newspaper out of my pocket, took off my coat, and folded it up. Then, I lay down on the floor and used the coat as a pillow.

I was really mad now. I was going to prison for the weekend. I wasn't just staying in a small jail cell at the police station. It wasn't like I had other plans, but I knew about regular prisons. Many soldiers who leave the army without permission end up in those prisons. For different reasons, the army gets told about it, and military police come to take them back. I had seen these prisons before, and I didn't like them. I lay there, annoyed, listening to the sounds of the police station. Phones were ringing, and keyboards were being used. The sounds went up and down. Officers were walking around, talking quietly.

Then, I tried to read the newspaper I had borrowed. It was full of nonsense about the president and his attempt to get re-elected for a second term. The president was in Pensacola, on the Gulf Coast. He wanted to balance the budget before his grandchildren got old. He was making big cuts, like someone cutting through the jungle with a knife. In Pensacola, he was making things difficult for the Coast Guard. For the past year, they had been watching the coast of Florida, stopping and searching any boats that looked suspicious. It had been announced with a lot of excitement, and it had been very successful. They had found many things, mostly drugs, but also guns and people who were trying to enter the country illegally from Haiti and Cuba. This had helped to reduce crime across the country.

But now, it was being stopped because it cost too much money. The Coast Guard was spending more than they had. The president said he couldn't give them more money; in fact, he had to give them less. The economy was in bad shape, and he couldn't do anything else. So, the program would end in a week. The president was trying to act like a leader, but law enforcement officials were angry because they thought it was better to prevent crime than to deal with it after it happened. People in Washington were happy because they thought spending money on local police was more noticeable to voters than spending money on the ocean far away. The arguments went back and forth, and in the photos, the president was just smiling like he was doing the right thing. I stopped reading because it was making me even angrier.

To calm myself down, I started thinking of music. I thought of the song "Smokestack Lightning." The version by Howling Wolf has a great scream at the end of the first line. People say you need to travel on trains to understand the blues, but that's not true. To understand the blues, you need to be stuck somewhere, like in a cell or in the army. Somewhere you're trapped. Somewhere where "smokestack lightning" seems like freedom that you can't reach. I lay there with my coat as a pillow and listened to the music in my head. At the end of the third chorus, I fell asleep.

I woke up again when Baker started hitting the bars of the cell. They made a dull sound, like a funeral bell. Baker and Finlay were standing there, looking down at me. I stayed on the floor because I was comfortable.

"Where did you say you were at midnight last night?" Finlay asked.

"Getting on the bus in Tampa," I said.

"We have a new person who saw what happened," Finlay said. "He saw you at the warehouse. Last night. Just standing around. At twelve o'clock at night."

"That's completely false, Finlay," I said. "That can't be. Who is this person who says they saw me?"

"The person who saw you is Chief Morrison," Finlay said. "He's the head of the police. He said he knew he had seen you somewhere before. Now he remembers where."
