I WAS ARRESTED AT ENO'S DINER. It was noon. I was having breakfast, eggs and coffee. It was late for breakfast, not quite lunch. I was wet and tired from walking a long way in the heavy rain. I had walked all the way from the highway to the edge of town.

The diner was small, but bright and clean. It looked brand new, like it was made to look like an old train car. It was long and narrow, with a counter on one side and a kitchen in the back. There were booths along the other wall, with a doorway in the middle where a booth would normally be.

I was sitting in a booth by the window, reading a newspaper someone had left behind. It was about the election, and I didn't vote for that president last time and wouldn't this time either. Outside, the rain had stopped, but the window was still covered in bright drops of water. I saw the police cars pull into the gravel parking lot. They were driving fast and stopped suddenly. The lights on top were flashing red and blue. The raindrops on my window glowed red and blue. The doors flew open, and police officers jumped out. Two from each car, with their weapons ready. Two had revolvers, and two had shotguns. This was serious. One with a revolver and one with a shotgun ran to the back. The other two rushed towards the door.

I just sat and watched them. I knew who was in the diner: a cook in the back, two waitresses, two old men, and me. They were here for me. I had only been in town for less than half an hour. The other five people had probably lived here their whole lives. If there was a problem with any of them, a police sergeant would have come in, looking embarrassed. He would have apologized and quietly asked them to come to the police station. So, the heavy weapons and the quick entrance weren't for them. They were for me. I quickly ate some egg, trapping a five-dollar bill under my plate. I folded the newspaper and put it in my coat pocket. I kept my hands on the table and finished my coffee.

The officer with the revolver stayed by the door. He crouched down and pointed his gun with both hands at my head. The officer with the shotgun came closer. They were both in good shape and looked professional. They were doing everything by the book. The officer with the revolver could shoot accurately across the room. The officer with the shotgun could easily shoot me at close range. Doing it the other way around would be a mistake. If the officer with the revolver missed during a struggle, a shotgun blast from the door could kill the officer, the old man in the back, and me. So far, they were doing everything right. They definitely had the advantage. I was stuck in the booth and couldn't move much. I put my hands on the table. The officer with the shotgun came closer.

"Freeze! Police!" he shouted.

He was shouting as loud as he could to release his stress and try to scare me. Textbook moves. Making a lot of noise to weaken the target. I raised my hands. The officer with the revolver started to come in from the door. The officer with the shotgun came even closer. Too close. That was their first mistake. If I had to, I could have grabbed the shotgun and pushed it upwards. Maybe it would fire into the ceiling, and I could hit the officer in the face with my elbow and take the shotgun. The officer with the revolver had moved to a position where he couldn't risk shooting his partner. It could have ended badly for them. But I just sat there with my hands up. The officer with the shotgun was still shouting and jumping around.

"Get out here on the floor!" he yelled.

I slowly got out of the booth and held out my wrists to the officer with the gun. I wasn't going to lie on the floor, not for these small-town cops, even if they brought the whole police force with big guns.

The officer with the gun was a sergeant. He was pretty calm. Another officer pointed a shotgun at me while the sergeant put his gun away, took out handcuffs, and put them on my wrists. The other officers came in through the kitchen. They walked around the counter and stood behind me. They searched me carefully. I saw the sergeant nod as they shook their heads. I didn't have a weapon.

The other officers each took one of my arms. The shotgun was still pointed at me. The sergeant stood in front of me. He was a fit, white man, about my age, with a tan. His name tag said "Baker." He looked up at me.

"You're arrested for murder," he said. "You have the right to stay silent. Anything you say can be used against you in court. You have the right to a lawyer. If you can't pay for a lawyer, the state of Georgia will give you one for free. Do you understand these rights?"

He said the Miranda rights perfectly. He spoke clearly and didn't read from a card. He sounded like he knew what the words meant and why they were important, both for him and for me. I didn't answer.

"Do you understand your rights?" he asked again.

Again, I didn't answer. I'd learned that being completely silent is the best thing to do. If you say something, it can be misheard, misunderstood, or misinterpreted. It can get you found guilty or even killed. Silence bothers the officer arresting you. He has to tell you that you have the right to be silent, but he doesn't like it when you use that right. I was being arrested for murder, but I didn't say anything.

"Do you understand your rights?" the officer, Baker, asked again. "Do you speak English?"

He was calm. I didn't say anything. He stayed calm. He was calm like someone who was no longer in danger. He would just drive me to the police station, and then I would be someone else's problem. He looked at the other three officers.

"Okay, write down that he didn't say anything," he said. "Let's go."

I walked toward the door. We lined up in a row at the door. Baker went first. Then the guy with the shotgun walked backward, pointing the big black barrel at me. His name tag said Stevenson. He was a white man, in good shape, and of average height. His gun looked like a pipe, pointing at my stomach. The other officers were behind me. Someone put a hand on my back and pushed me through the door.

Outside in the gravel parking lot, it was hot. It must have rained all night and most of the morning. Now the sun was strong, and the ground was steaming. Usually, it was a dusty, hot place. But today, it smelled great, like wet pavement under the hot sun. I looked up at the sun and took a deep breath as the officers got organized. One officer stood on each side of me as we walked to the cars. Stevenson still held the shotgun. At the first car, he stepped back as Baker opened the back door. Someone pushed my head down and helped me into the car. The officer on the left bumped me with his hip, guiding me in. They moved well. In a small town like this, they probably trained a lot.

I was alone in the back of the car. A thick glass wall separated the front and back seats. The front doors were still open. Baker and Stevenson got in. Baker drove. Stevenson turned around to watch me. No one spoke. The other police car followed us. The cars were new, quiet, and comfortable. They were clean and cool inside. It didn't feel like desperate, sad people had been in the car before.

I looked out the window at Georgia. The land looked rich with heavy, red, damp soil. There were very long, straight rows of low bushes in the fields. Maybe they were peanuts. They were grown close to the ground but valuable to the farmers, or the landowners. Did people own their land here, or did big companies? I didn't know.

The drive to town was short. The car moved quietly on the smooth, wet road. After about a half mile, I saw two nice, new buildings with well-kept lawns. The police station and the fire station. They stood together behind a large lawn with a statue, at the north edge of town. They were attractive buildings that were well-funded. The roads were smooth, and the sidewalks were red blocks. About 300 yards south, I could see a bright white church steeple behind a small group of buildings. I saw flagpoles, awnings, fresh paint, and green lawns. Everything looked fresh from the rain and somehow intense in the heat. It was a prosperous community. I guessed it was built on good farm incomes and high taxes from people who worked in Atlanta.

Stevenson still stared at me as the car slowed down to turn into the police station. It had a wide, semicircular driveway. I read on a sign: Margrave Police Headquarters. I wondered if I should be worried. I was under arrest in a town I'd never been to before, apparently for murder. But I knew two things. First, they couldn't prove something that didn't happen. And second, I hadn't killed anyone.

Not in their town, and not for a long time, anyway.
