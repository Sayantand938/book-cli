## Chapter 1

They learned about him in July and stayed mad all through August. They tried to kill him in September, but it was too early. They weren't ready, and their attempt failed. It could have been terrible, but it was actually lucky because no one even noticed.

They used their normal method to get past security and got close to him while he was talking. They used a silencer on their gun and missed him by a tiny bit. The bullet must have flown right over his head, maybe even through his hair. He immediately raised his hand and smoothed his hair back like it was messed up by the wind. They watched it again and again on TV later. He raised his hand and fixed his hair, but did nothing else. He just kept talking, not knowing anything had happened because a silenced bullet is too fast to see and too quiet to hear. So, it missed him and everyone behind him. It didn't hit anything, no people or buildings. It flew straight until it lost power and fell to the ground far away in an empty field. No one responded. No one reacted. Nobody noticed a thing. It was like the bullet was never fired. They didn't shoot again because they were too shocked. So, it was a failure, but also a miracle, and they learned something from it. They spent October acting like professionals. They started over, calmed down, thought things through, learned from their mistakes, and prepared for their second try. It would be better planned and done correctly, using skill and cleverness, and driven by fear. A worthwhile try. A creative try. Most importantly, a try that wouldn't fail.

Then, November came, and everything changed. Reacher's coffee cup was empty but still warm. He picked it up, tilted it, and watched the coffee grounds at the bottom slowly slide toward him, like brown mud.

"A handshake isn't enough," she said. "You're going to do this for us." She paused. "And you were almost my brother-in-law."

He didn't say anything. He just nodded, moved away from the table, and glanced back once. Then he went up the stairs and out to the street. Her perfume was on his hand. He had worked there for twenty-five years and was close to retirement. He was in his middle fifties, a reminder of the old days. "No, I'm sure. I don't see any other choice."

Not someone who had been a major in the US Army and had medals.

Reacher remembered his mom saying, "Please don't fight when you have new clothes." Then he remembered a drill sergeant saying, "Hit them fast, hit them hard, and hit them a lot." He moved his shoulders inside his coat. He was happy he got the bigger size. He looked at the two guys, showing nothing but a little amusement and confidence. He moved a bit to his left, and they moved with him. He moved closer, making the space smaller. He raised his hand and fixed his hair in the wind.

"It's better to just walk away now," he said.

They didn't, as he knew they wouldn't. They moved closer, just a little, shifting their weight forward. He thought, "They need to be in bed for a week." Broken cheekbones, maybe. A hard hit, maybe they'll pass out for a bit and get headaches. Nothing too bad. He waited for the wind again and put his right hand behind his left ear. He left his elbow up, like he just had an idea.

"Can you guys swim?" he asked.

They couldn't help but look at the ocean. They weren't that strong. They turned their heads like robots. He hit the guy on the right in the face with his elbow and then hit the guy on the left as his head turned back. They fell to the ground together, their coins scattering everywhere. Reacher coughed in the cold and thought, "Two guys, two seconds, two hits, done." He thought, "I've still got it." He breathed hard and wiped sweat from his forehead. Then he walked away. He stepped off the pier onto the boardwalk and looked for Western Union. He saw the address in the motel book, but he didn't need it. You can just feel where it is. Simple: ask yourself, "Is it more likely to be left or right?" Then you turn that way, and you find it. This one had a black Chevy truck parked illegally outside. The truck was clean with dark windows and three antennas on top. A woman was in the driver's seat. He looked at her quickly, then again. She had blonde hair and looked relaxed but alert. Her arm was resting on the window. And she was cute, for sure. She had something about her. He looked away and got his money from inside. He put it in his pocket and came back out. The woman was standing in front of him, looking at his face, like she was comparing it to a picture in her head. He knew that look. He had seen it before.

"Jack Reacher?" she asked.

He quickly thought back, because he wanted to be sure, even though he was pretty sure he was right. Short, light-colored hair, amazing eyes looking straight at him, and a calm confidence in how she stood. She had qualities he would usually remember. He was certain of that. But he didn't remember them. So, he'd never seen her before.

"You knew my brother," he said.

She looked surprised and a bit pleased. For a moment, she didn't know what to say.

"I could tell," he said. "When people look at me like that, they're thinking about how much we look alike, but also how different we are."

She didn't say anything.

"It was nice to meet you," he said, and started to walk away.

"Wait," she called out.

He turned around.

"Can we talk?" she asked. "I've been searching for you."

He nodded. "We can talk in the car. I'm really cold out here." She paused, looking closely at his face. Then, she quickly moved and opened the car door on the passenger side.

"Please, get in," she said. He got in, and she walked around the front of the car to the driver's side and got in herself. She started the engine to turn on the heat, but she didn't drive.

"I knew your brother very well," she said. "Joe and I were a couple. We were more than just dating. We were very serious for a while, before he passed away."

Reacher didn't say anything. The woman's face turned red.

"Well, of course, it was before he died," she said. "That was a silly thing to say." She became quiet.

"When was this?" Reacher asked.

"We were together for two years. We broke up a year before he died."

Reacher nodded.

"My name is M. E. Froelich," she said.

She seemed to be asking without saying it: Did he ever talk about me? Reacher nodded again, trying to act like the name meant something to him. But it didn't. I've never heard of you, he thought. But maybe I wish I had.

"Emmy?" he asked. "Like the TV award?"

"M. E.," she answered. "I use my initials."

"What do they stand for?"

"I'm not going to tell you."

He stopped for a moment. "What did Joe call you?"

"He called me Froelich," she said.

He agreed. "Yes, he would."

"I still miss him," she said.

"I miss him too, I think," Reacher said. "So, is this about Joe, or is it about something else?"

She became still again for a moment. Then she shook herself slightly and became all business.

"Both," she answered. "Actually, it's mostly something else."

"Do you want to tell me what it is?"

"I want to hire you for a job," she said. "Joe suggested you, even though he's gone. He used to talk about you and what you could do."

Reacher nodded. "Hire me to do what?"

Froelich stopped for a moment and then smiled slightly.

"I've practiced saying this," she said. "A few times."

"So, tell me."

"I want you to kill the Vice President of the United States."
