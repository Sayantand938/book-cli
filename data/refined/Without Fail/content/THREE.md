## Chapter 3

She drove her SUV back to Washington, D.C., arguing with herself the whole time. If the news is very bad, when should she tell Stuyvesant? Now? Later? Finally, she stopped her car at Dupont Circle, called him at home, and asked him directly.

"I'll help when I need to," he answered. "Who did you hire?"

"Joe Reacher's brother."

"Our Joe Reacher? I didn't know he had a brother."

"Well, he does."

"What's he like?"

"Like Joe, but maybe a bit tougher."

"Is he older or younger?"

"Both," Froelich answered. "He started younger, but now he's older."

Stuyvesant was quiet for a second. "Is he as smart as Joe?" he asked.

"I don't know yet," Froelich replied.

Stuyvesant was quiet again. "Call me when you need me. But call me soon, okay? And don't tell anyone else about this."

She hung up and moved back into the busy Sunday traffic. She drove the last mile and parked in front of the hotel. The front desk was expecting her and told her to go straight up to room 1201 on the twelfth floor. She followed a waiter inside. He had a tray with a coffee pot and two cups turned upside down on saucers. There was no milk, sugar, or spoons, just a single pink rose in a small vase. The room looked like any other city hotel room. It had two queen-sized beds, curtains with flower patterns, simple pictures on the walls, a table, two chairs, a desk with a complicated phone, a cabinet with a TV, and a door that connected to the next room.

Reacher was sitting on the bed closest to the door. He was wearing a black nylon jacket, a black T-shirt, black jeans, and black shoes. He had an earpiece in his ear and a pretty good fake Secret Service badge on his jacket collar. He had shaved and his hair was cut short and neatly combed.

"What do you have for me?" she asked.

"Later," he said.

The waiter put the tray on the table and quietly left the room. Froelich watched the door close and then turned back to Reacher. She stopped for a moment.

"You look just like one of us," she said.

He said, "You owe me a lot of money."

"Twenty thousand dollars?"

He smiled. "Almost that much. Did they tell you about it?"

She nodded. "But why a cashier's check? That confused me."

"It won't confuse you for long."

He stood up and walked over to the table. He straightened the cups, picked up the coffee pot, and poured coffee.

"You ordered room service at just the right time," she said.

He smiled again. "I knew where you were and that you'd be driving back. It's Sunday, so there's no traffic. It was easy to guess when you'd arrive."

"So, what do you want to tell me?"

"That you're good," he said. "That you're really, really good. I don't think anyone else could do this as well as you."

She became silent. "But?"

"But you're not skilled enough. You need to understand that someone else could easily come in and finish the job."

"I didn't say anyone else was involved." He didn't reply. "Just give me the information, Reacher."

"Three and a half," he said.

"Three and a half what? Out of ten?"

"No, Armstrong is way beyond dead, like three and a half times." She looked at him, shocked. "Already?"

"That's how I see it," he answered.

"What do you mean by a half?"

"Three for sure, and one maybe."

She stopped walking to the table and just stood there, confused.

"In five days?" she asked. "How can we do it so fast? What are we skipping?"

"Here, have some coffee," he said.

She walked to the table like a robot. He gave her a cup. She took it and moved back to the bed. The cup shook against the saucer.

"There are two main ways to do this," Reacher said. "Like in the movies, with John Malkovich or Edward Fox. Have you seen those movies?"

She nodded, looking confused. "We have a guy who watches movies. He works in the Office of Protection Research. He studies all the movies about killing people. John Malkovich was in 'In the Line of Fire' with Clint Eastwood."

"And Rene Russo," Reacher added. "She was good too."

"Edward Fox was in 'The Day of the Jackal,' a long time ago."

Reacher agreed. "John Malkovich wanted to kill the President of the United States, and Edward Fox wanted to kill the President of France. Both were good killers working alone. But there was a big difference between them. John Malkovich knew he wouldn't survive. He knew he would die right after the President. But Edward Fox planned to escape."

"But he didn't get away."

"It was just a movie, Froelich. It had to end that way. He could have easily escaped in real life."

"So, what's the point?"

"It gives us two possible plans. Either get very close for a suicide attack, or stay far away and do it cleanly."

"We already know that. I told you, we have someone working on it. We get copies of everything, like reports, notes, and official papers. We even talk to the writers sometimes, if there's something new. We want to know where they get their ideas."

"Have you learned anything?"

She shrugged, took a sip of her coffee, and seemed to be searching her memory. It was like she had all the reports and notes stored in her head.

"I think 'The Day of the Jackal' made an impression on us," she said. "Edward Fox played a professional killer who had a rifle made to look like a crutch for a disabled person. He used this disguise to get into a building before the target's public appearance and planned to shoot from a window on a high floor, from far away. He was using a silencer, so he could escape. It could have worked, in theory. But that story happened a long time ago, before I was born. In the early Sixties, I think. It was about General de Gaulle, after the Algerian crisis, right? Now, we have much bigger security zones. I guess that movie played a part in that. Plus, we had our own problems back in the early Sixties, of course."

"And what about 'In the Line of Fire'?" Reacher asked.

"John Malkovich played a CIA agent who had gone bad," she said. "He built a plastic gun in his basement so he could get past the metal detectors. He tricked his way into a campaign event and planned to shoot the President from very close. As you said, we would have stopped him right away."

"But Clint Eastwood jumped in front of the bullet," Reacher said. "I thought it was a good movie."

"We thought it was not realistic," Froelich replied. "Two main problems. First, the idea that you can build a working gun from hobby materials is ridiculous. We look at things like that all the time. His gun would have exploded and hurt him. The bullet would have just fallen out. Second, he spent a lot of money, about a hundred thousand dollars. He traveled a lot, used fake offices to receive mail, and donated fifty thousand dollars to the party to get into the campaign event. We figured a crazy person like that wouldn't have that much money to spend. So, we didn't think it was likely."

"It was just a movie," Reacher explained. "But it showed something important."

"Showed what?"

"It showed how someone might attack a target up close during a gathering, instead of staying far away for safety."

Froelich stopped for a moment. Then she smiled, carefully at first, as if a big threat was disappearing.

"Is that all you have?" she asked. "Just ideas? You had me worried."

"Like the gathering here on Thursday night," Reacher said. "A thousand people will be there. The time and place are known ahead of time. It's even advertised."

"You found the Transition group's website?"

Reacher nodded. "It was very helpful. It had a lot of information."

"We check everything on it."

"But it still told me everywhere Armstrong will be," Reacher said. "And when. And what he'll be doing. Like the gathering right here, Thursday night. With a thousand people."

"What about those people?"

"I heard that one of them, a woman with dark hair, grabbed Armstrong's hand and made him lose his balance a bit."

She looked at him closely. "You saw this happen?"

He shook his head. "No, but I heard about it from someone."

"How did you hear?"

He didn't answer her question. "Did you see it happen?"

"Only in a video," she said. "After it already happened."

"That woman could have killed Armstrong. That was the first chance anyone had. Before that, you were doing a great job. You were getting top marks for your work with the government around the Capitol building."

She smiled slightly, as if she didn't think much of what he said. "Could have? You're wasting my time, Reacher. I wanted you to tell me something more definite than 'could have.' I mean, anything is possible. Lightning could strike the building. Even a meteorite could hit us. The universe could even start shrinking instead of growing, and time could go backwards. That woman was invited to be there. She donated money to the party, she walked through two metal detectors, and they checked her ID at the door."

"Like John Malkovich."

"We already talked about that."

"Let's say she knew martial arts, like a soldier trained in secret missions. She could have snapped Armstrong's neck as easily as you break a pencil."

"Let's say, let's say."

"Let's say she had a weapon."

"She didn't. She walked through two metal detectors."

Reacher reached into his jacket pocket and pulled out something small and brown.

"Have you ever seen this?" he asked.

It looked like a small knife, about three and a half inches long, with a curved handle. He pressed a button, and a brown blade popped out.

"This is made of ceramic," he said. "It's like the material in bathroom tiles. It's stronger and sharper than steel. And it won't set off a metal detector. The woman could have had this with her. She could have cut Armstrong from his stomach to his chin, or slashed his throat, or stabbed him in the eye."

He handed the knife to Froelich, who took it and looked at it closely.

"It's made by a company called Beker," Reacher explained. "They're in Solingen, Germany. They're pricey, but you can find them without too much trouble."

Froelich just shrugged. "Okay, so you bought a knife. That doesn't prove anything."

"That knife was in the ballroom on Thursday night. That woman held it in her left hand, in her pocket, with the blade open. She held it like that while she was shaking Armstrong's hand and pulling him close. His stomach was only a few inches away from the blade."

Froelich looked shocked. "Are you serious? Who was she?"

"She was a campaign supporter named Elizabeth Wright. She's from Elizabeth, New Jersey, actually. She donated four thousand dollars to the campaign, a thousand in her name, her husband's, and each of her two kids' names. She helped prepare mail for a month, put up a big sign in her yard, and made calls on election day."

"So why would she carry a knife?"

"Well, the truth is, she didn't."

He stood up and went to the door between the rooms. He pulled his side open a little and knocked loudly on the other side.

"Okay, Neagley," he called out. The door opened, and a woman came in from the next room. She looked to be in her late thirties, medium height and thin, wearing jeans and a soft gray sweatshirt. She had dark hair, dark eyes, and a great smile. The way she moved and the muscles in her wrists showed she spent a lot of time at the gym. "You're the woman in the video," Froelich said.

Reacher smiled. "Frances Neagley, this is M. E. Froelich. M. E. Froelich, this is Frances Neagley."

"Emmy?" Frances Neagley asked. "Like the TV award?"

"Just her initials," Reacher explained.

Froelich looked at him, confused. "Who is she?"

"The best master sergeant I ever worked with. She's really good at all kinds of hand-to-hand fighting. She even scares me. She left the army around the same time I did. Now she works as a security advisor in Chicago."

"Chicago," Froelich said again. "So that's why the check was sent there."

Reacher agreed. "She paid for everything because I don't have a credit card or a checkbook. You probably already knew that."

"So what happened to Elizabeth Wright from New Jersey?"

"I bought these clothes," Reacher said. "Or, you bought them for me. And the shoes, and the sunglasses. This is my version of a Secret Service uniform. I got a haircut and shaved every day. I wanted to look believable. Then I needed a woman from New Jersey, so I went to the airport here on Thursday and watched people get off flights from Newark. I picked Ms. Wright out of the crowd and told her I was a Secret Service agent. I said there was a big security problem and she needed to come with me."

"How did you know she was going to the rally?"

"I didn't. I just looked at all the women who came to pick up their bags and tried to guess based on how they looked and what they were carrying. It wasn't easy. Elizabeth Wright was the sixth woman I spoke to."

"And she actually believed you?"

"I had really good fake ID. I bought this small radio for my ear from Radio Shack for only two dollars. It had a thin wire that went down my neck, see? I also rented a fancy black car. I looked like the real deal, trust me. She totally fell for it. She was really excited about the whole thing. I brought her back to this room and watched over her all night while Neagley took over. I kept listening to my earpiece and talking into my watch."

Froelich then looked at Neagley.

"We chose New Jersey for a reason," Neagley explained. "Their driver's licenses are the easiest to fake, you know? I had my laptop and a color printer with me. I had just finished making Reacher's fake Secret Service ID. I had no idea if it looked like the real thing, but it looked pretty good. So, I made a fake New Jersey license with my picture and the name and address on it. I printed it, put plastic over it using a machine we bought from Staples for sixty dollars, smoothed the edges with sandpaper, roughed it up a bit, and put it in my bag. Then, I dressed nicely, took Ms. Wright's party invitation, and went downstairs. I got into the ballroom without any problems. And I had the knife in my pocket."

"And then what?"

"I waited around, then I grabbed your guy's hand. I held on for a while."

Froelich looked right at her. "How would you have done it?"

"I held his right hand with my right hand. I pulled him close, he turned a little, and I had a clear shot at the right side of his neck. With my three-and-a-half-inch knife, I would have stabbed him in his carotid artery. Then, I would have twisted it around. He would have bled to death in about thirty seconds. I was just one move away from doing it. Your people were only ten feet away. They would have shot me right after, but they couldn't have stopped me from killing him."

Froelich was pale and quiet. Neagley looked away.

"Without the knife, it would have been harder," she said. "But not impossible. Breaking his neck would have been difficult because he's pretty strong. I would have had to move quickly to throw him off balance, and if your people were fast enough, they might have stopped me. So, I guess I would have hit him hard in the throat, hard enough to crush it. A quick jab with my left elbow would have done it. I probably would have been killed before he died, but he would have suffocated right after, unless you have people who could do an emergency surgery on the ballroom floor in a minute or so, which I'm guessing you don't."

"No," Froelich said. "We don't have one." She stopped talking again.

"Sorry to make your day worse," Neagley said. "But you wanted to know this information, didn't you? There's no use checking security if we don't tell you what we found."

Froelich nodded. "What did you say quietly to him?"

"I said, 'I have a knife,' just for fun. I said it very quietly. If anyone asked, I would have said I asked, 'Where's your wife?' Like I was flirting with him. I guess that happens sometimes."

Froelich nodded again. "It does," she said. "Sometimes. What else?"

"Well, he's safe in his house," Neagley said.

"Did you make sure?"

"Every day," Reacher said. "We've been in Georgetown since Tuesday night."

"I didn't see you."

"That's what we wanted."

"How did you find out where he lives?"

"We followed your fancy cars," Froelich didn't answer. "Nice cars," Reacher said. "Smooth moves."

"Friday morning went really well," Neagley said.

"But the rest of Friday was pretty bad," Reacher said. "You weren't working together well, and that caused a big mistake in communication."

"Where?"

"Your people in Washington D.C. had a video of the ballroom, but your people in New York clearly didn't see it. Neagley was the woman in the party dress on Thursday night, and she was also one of the photographers outside the Stock Exchange."

"A newspaper in North Dakota has a website," Neagley said. "Like all of them, it has a picture of its name. I downloaded it and changed it to make it look like a press pass. I covered it in plastic and made holes in it, then hung it around my neck with a nylon cord. I looked through used stores in lower Manhattan for old, beat-up camera equipment. I held a camera in front of my face the whole time so Armstrong wouldn't recognize me."

"You should have a list of who's allowed in," Reacher said. "And control who gets on it somehow."

"We can't," Froelich said. "It's because of the Constitution. The First Amendment says journalists can go anywhere they want, whenever they want. But they were all searched."

"I wasn't carrying a weapon," Neagley said. "I was just breaking your security for fun. But I could have been carrying one, that's for sure. I could have gotten a bazooka past that kind of search."

Reacher stood up and walked to the cabinet. He opened a drawer and took out a pile of photos. They were regular color prints that you can get in an hour. He held up the first photo. It was taken from a low angle and showed Armstrong standing in front of the Stock Exchange. The words carved above the entrance looked like a halo over his head.

"Neagley took this," Reacher said. "I thought it was a good picture. Maybe we could sell it to a magazine and make back some of the twenty thousand dollars we spent."

He walked back to the bed, sat down, and handed the photo to Froelich. She took it and looked at it carefully.

"The point is, I was only four feet away," Neagley said. "I could have reached him if I wanted to. Like that movie with John Malkovich, but whatever."

Froelich just nodded, looking confused. Reacher gave her the next photo like he was dealing a card. It was a blurry picture taken from far away, looking down from high above the street. It showed Armstrong outside the Stock Exchange, very small in the center. Someone had drawn a circle around his head with a pen, like aiming a gun.

"This one's the half," Reacher said. "I was on the sixtieth floor of an office building three hundred yards away. I was inside the area the police had blocked off, but higher up than they were checking."

"With a rifle?"

He shook his head. "I used a piece of wood the same size and shape as a rifle. And another camera, of course. And a big lens. But I acted like it was real. I wanted to see if it was possible. I thought people would notice a rifle, so I got a big square box from a computer screen and put the wood in it, going from one corner to the other. Then I just rolled it into the elevator on a cart, pretending it was very heavy. I saw a few cops. I was wearing these clothes, but without the fake pin or the earpiece. I guess they thought I was a delivery guy or something. It was a Friday, after the stock market closed, so the area was getting quiet. I found a window in an empty meeting room. It wouldn't open, so I guess I would have had to cut a hole in the glass. But I could have taken a shot, just like I took the picture. And I would have gotten away clean."

Froelich nodded, but she seemed unsure. "Why only a half?" she asked. "It looks like you had a clear shot."

"Not in Manhattan," Reacher said. "I was about nine hundred feet away and six hundred feet up. That's like shooting eleven hundred feet, more or less. It's not a problem for me usually, but the wind and the hot air around those buildings make it unpredictable. They're always changing, every second. They swirl around, going up and down and side to side. You can't be sure you'll hit anything. That's the good thing, really. No good shooter would try a long shot in Manhattan. Only an idiot would, and an idiot would miss anyway."

Froelich nodded, feeling a bit better. "Okay," she said.

_She's not worried about someone clumsy,_ Reacher thought. _It must be a skilled person._

"So," he said. "Let's just say we found three things in total, and forget about the half thing. And don't worry about what happened in New York. It wasn't very important."

"But what we found in Bismarck _was_ important," Neagley said. "We arrived there around midnight. We took regular flights, going through Chicago."

"I called you from about a mile away," Reacher said. "About the musicians."

He put down the next two photos.

"These were taken with infrared film," he said. "In the dark."

The first picture showed the back of the Armstrong family's house. The colors looked strange because of the infrared film. But it was a pretty close picture. You could see everything clearly: the doors, the windows. Froelich could even see one of her people standing in the yard.

"Where were you when you took this?" she asked.

"On the neighbor's land," Reacher said. "Maybe fifty feet away. It was a simple night move, sneaking in during the dark. Basic soldier skills, quiet and careful. A couple of dogs barked a little, but we avoided them. The police in their cars didn't see anything."

Neagley pointed to the second photo. It showed the front of the house. The colors, details, and distance were the same as the first photo.

"I was across the street, in front of the house," she said. "I was hiding behind someone's garage."

Reacher leaned forward on the bed. "The plan would have been to use M16 rifles with grenade launchers. We would have also had other automatic rifles. Maybe even M60 machine guns on stands. We had plenty of time to set everything up. We would have shot phosphorus grenades into the building with the M16s, at the same time from the front and back. One grenade on the ground floor at each location. Either Armstrong would burn in his bed, or we would shoot him as he ran out the door or jumped out the window. We would have done it around four in the morning. It would have been a complete shock. There would have been total confusion. We could have easily killed your agents with the M16s. We could have destroyed the entire house. We probably would have escaped okay. Then it would have become a normal search for us. It wouldn't have been ideal out there in the countryside, but we probably would have made it with some luck. Edward Fox again."

It was silent.

"I don't believe it," Froelich said. She looked at the pictures. "This can't be from Friday night. This must have been some other night. You weren't really there."

Reacher didn't say anything.

"Were you?" she asked.

"Well, look at this," Reacher said. He gave her another photo. It was taken with a telephoto lens. It showed her sitting in the apartment window above the garage, looking out into the dark, holding her cell phone. Her heat signature was shown in red, orange, and purple colors. But it was her. There was no question. It looked like she was close enough to touch.

"I was calling New Jersey," she said quietly. "Your musician friends got away safely."

"Good," Reacher said. "Thanks for taking care of it." She looked at the three infrared pictures, one after the other, and said nothing.

"So, the ballroom and the house were definitely targets," Reacher stated. "The bad guys scored two points. But the real proof came the next day, yesterday, at the rally at the church."

He slid the last picture over. It was a normal photo, taken from above. It showed Armstrong in his thick coat walking on the community center's grass. The afternoon sun made a long shadow behind him. He was walking with a small group of people, but his head was easy to see. Someone had drawn a rough target around it. "I was in the church tower," Reacher said. "The church was locked."

"At eight in the morning. I had been there since five."

"It was searched."

"I was up where the bells are, at the top of a wooden ladder, behind a hidden door. I put pepper on the ladder. The dogs lost interest and stayed downstairs."

"It was a local team. They made mistakes."

"I considered stopping the event."

"You should have."

"Then I thought about telling him to wear a bulletproof vest."

"It wouldn't have helped. I would have aimed for his head. It was a perfect day, Froelich. Clear, sunny, no wind. Cool, thick air. Perfect conditions. I was a couple hundred feet away. I could have shot him in the eyes."

She became silent.

"John Malkovich or Edward Fox?" she questioned.

"I would have shot Armstrong first, and then as many other people as I could in a few seconds. Probably police officers, but also women and children. I would have tried to injure them, not kill them. Aim for the stomach, most likely. It's more effective that way. People would be falling and bleeding everywhere, causing a big panic. That would be enough for me to escape. I'd break out of the church in about ten seconds and quickly disappear into the nearby neighborhood. Neagley would be waiting in a car. She would drive off as soon as she heard the shots. So, I guess I would have been Edward Fox."

Froelich stood up and walked to the window. She placed her hands flat on the windowsill and looked out at the weather. "This is a complete failure," she said. Reacher didn't respond.

"I suppose I didn't expect how focused you are," she said. "I didn't realize it would be like a full-blown war."

Reacher shrugged. "Assassins aren't usually the nicest people. And they make the rules in this situation."

Froelich agreed. "And I didn't know you would get help, especially from a woman."

"I did try to warn you," Reacher said. "I told you it wouldn't work if you were waiting for me. You can't expect assassins to announce their plans beforehand."

"I know," she said. "But I was only picturing one man."

"It's always going to be a team," Reacher said. "There's no such thing as a lone man."

He saw a faint, mocking smile in the reflection.

"So, you don't trust the Warren Report?" she questioned.

He shook his head. "You don't either," he replied. "No expert ever will."

"I don't feel like much of an expert today," she answered.

Neagley stood up, walked over, and sat on the edge of the window next to Froelich, leaning back against the glass.

"Think about the situation," she said. "It's not that bad. Reacher and I used to be specialists in the Army's Criminal Investigation Division. We had all sorts of training. Mostly, we were trained to think. To be creative. And definitely to be tough and sure of ourselves. Also, we had to be stronger than the people we watched, and some of them were really tough. So, we're very different. There aren't many people as specialized as us, maybe only ten thousand in the whole country."

"Ten thousand is a lot," Froelich said.

"Compared to 281 million people? And how many of those are the right age, available, and want to do this job? It's a really small number. So, don't worry too much. Because you have an impossible job anyway. You have to let him be open to danger because he's a politician. He has to do things that people can see. We would never have allowed anyone to do what Armstrong does. Never. It would have been completely unthinkable."

Froelich turned to face the room. She swallowed and nodded slightly, looking off into the distance.

"Thanks," she said. "For trying to help me feel better. But I need to think about things now, right?"

"Set up security," Reacher said. "Make the security area half a mile wide around him. Keep the public away, and always have at least four security people close enough to touch him. That's the best you can do."

Froelich shook her head. "We can't do that," she said. "People would think it's unfair and not in line with democracy. And we'll have hundreds of weeks like this for the next three years. After that, it will get even harder because they'll be in their last year and trying to get re-elected, so security will have to be even less strict. And in about seven years, Armstrong will start trying to get the nomination himself. Have you seen how they do that? Big crowds everywhere, starting in New Hampshire? Meetings in small towns? Fundraising events? It's a total mess."

The room became silent. Neagley got off the window and walked to the cabinet. She took two thin files from the drawer where the photos had been. She held up the first one.

"This is a report," she said. "It has the important points and our advice, from a professional point of view."

"Okay," Froelich said.

Neagley held up the second file.

"And this is for our costs," she said. "Everything is listed, with receipts. Make the check out to Reacher. It was his money."

"Okay," Froelich said again. She took the files and held them close, as if they would protect her from something.

"And there's Elizabeth Wright from New Jersey," Reacher said. "Don't forget about her. She needs to be taken care of. I told her that to make up for missing the party you'd probably invite her to the Inauguration Ball."

"Okay," Froelich said for the third time. "The Ball, whatever. I'll talk to someone about it."

Then she just stopped moving.

"This is a mess," she repeated.

"You have a really hard job," Reacher said. "Don't blame yourself too much."

She agreed. "Joe used to tell me the same thing. He said that, considering the situation, we should be happy with a ninety-five percent success rate."

"Ninety-four percent," Reacher corrected. "You've lost one president out of eighteen since your team started. That's a six percent failure rate. That's not so bad."

"Ninety-four, ninety-five," she said. "Whatever, I guess he was right."

"Joe was right about many things, as I remember."

"But we've never lost a vice president," she said. "Not yet." She put the files under her arm and arranged the photos on the side table, pushing them with her fingers until they were in a neat stack. She picked them up and put them in her bag. Then she looked at each wall, as if trying to remember exactly how they looked. It was a small, absent-minded action. She nodded to herself and walked towards the door.

"I have to leave," she said.

She left the room, and the door closed tightly behind her. It was quiet for a moment. Then Neagley stood up straight at the end of one of the beds. She grabbed the ends of her sweatshirt sleeves, held them in her hands, and stretched her arms above her head. She looked up and yawned. Her hair fell down over her shoulders. Her shirt went up a little, and Reacher saw strong muscles above her jeans. They looked like the shell of a turtle.

"You still look good," he commented.

"You too, in that black outfit."

"It feels like a uniform," he replied. "It's been five years since I last wore one."

Neagley finished stretching. She smoothed her hair and tucked her shirt back into place.

"Are we finished here?" she asked. "Are you tired?"

"Yes, very tired. We worked really hard and made that woman's day difficult."

"What did you think about her?"

"I liked her. And like I told her, I think her job is impossible. But I also think she's doing a good job. I don't think anyone else could do it better. And I think she knows that too, but she's frustrated that she can only do 95% instead of 100%."

"I agree."

"Who's this guy Joe she mentioned?"

"An old boyfriend of yours."

"You knew him?"

"He's my brother. She went out with him."

"When was that?"

"They split up six years ago."

"What's he like?"
